The Language and Logic of Geometry

Lesson I

Geometry is among the oldest branches of mathematics, and perhaps the most elegant. Beginning with a small set of self-evident truths, geometers have constructed an edifice of knowledge through pure logical reasoning. This lesson introduces the primitive objects—points, lines, rays, and angles—and demonstrates how mathematical proof transforms assumptions into certainty.

Section 1

The Nature of Geometry

Geometry is an axiomatic system: we begin with a small number of statements accepted without proof, called axioms or postulates, and derive all other truths through logical deduction.

This method was perfected around 300 BCE by the Greek mathematician Euclid, whose treatise The Elements remained the standard text on geometry for over two thousand years. The figure below shows Proposition 1 from Book I: the construction of an equilateral triangle using only a compass and straightedge.

Every theorem we prove rests upon earlier theorems, which rest upon still earlier ones, all the way back to the axioms. This is the power of deductive reasoning: once we accept the axioms, the rest follows with certainty.

Section 2

Primitive Objects

Point
That which has position but no magnitude. Denoted by capital letters: A, B, P.
Line
Extends infinitely in both directions. A line through points A and B is written AB with a line symbol above, or simply "line AB."
Ray
A half-line: begins at a point (its endpoint) and extends infinitely in one direction. Ray AB starts at A and passes through B.
Segment
The set of all points between two endpoints, including the endpoints themselves. Segment AB has finite length.

Figure 2.1 — The four primitive objects, distinguished by their extent

Section 3

The Angle

An angle is the figure formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint. This shared point is called the vertex; the two rays are called the sides of the angle.

Vertex
The common endpoint of the two rays forming the angle.
Sides
The two rays emanating from the vertex.

The measure of an angle quantifies the rotation required to bring one side into coincidence with the other. We shall see that this measure is expressed in degrees.

Figure 3.1 — Anatomy of an angle

Section 4

Degree Measure

The degree (°) is the standard unit of angular measure. A full rotation comprises 360°, a convention dating to ancient Babylonian astronomy.

Figure 4.1 — Classification of angles by measure

Acute Angle
An angle measuring less than 90°.
Right Angle
An angle measuring exactly 90°. Denoted by a small square at the vertex.
Obtuse Angle
An angle measuring greater than 90° but less than 180°.
Straight Angle
An angle measuring exactly 180°. The sides form a line.
Section 5

Notation for Angles

An angle is named using three points: one on each side and one at the vertex. The symbol ∠ denotes "angle."

Convention
The middle letter always denotes the vertex.

Thus ∠ABC refers to the angle at vertex B, with one side passing through A and the other through C.

Remark

The notation ∠CBA describes the same angle as ∠ABC, since both have vertex at B. The order of the outer letters is immaterial.

Section 6

The Linear Pair Postulate

As discussed, geometry proceeds from axioms—statements accepted without proof. We now introduce our first postulate concerning angles.

Postulate (Linear Pair)
If two angles form a linear pair (that is, their non-common sides form a straight line), then the sum of their measures is 180°.

Figure 6.1 — Angles ∠ABD and ∠DBC form a linear pair

Supplementary Angles
Two angles are said to be supplementary if the sum of their measures equals 180°.

Corollary. Any two angles forming a linear pair are supplementary.

Section 7

Worked Examples

Example 1

Problem. Two angles form a linear pair. One measures 70°. Find the other.

Solution. By the Linear Pair Postulate, the angles sum to 180°. Thus:

180° − 70° = 110°

Example 2

Problem. Two right angles form a linear pair. Verify the postulate.

Solution. Each angle measures 90°. Their sum:

90° + 90° = 180°  ✓

Summary of Angle Classification

Acute: 0° < θ < 90°   |   Right: θ = 90°   |   Obtuse: 90° < θ < 180°   |   Straight: θ = 180°
